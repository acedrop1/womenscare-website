When should my teenage daughter see a gynecologist?

ACOG recommends the first visit between ages 13 and 15. Here's what happens, what doesn't, and how to prepare.

If you are a parent wondering when, how, or whether to bring your daughter to a gynecologist for the first time, here is a clear guide based on professional recommendations.

The recommended age

The American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG) recommends the first gynecology visit between ages 13 and 15.

Source: ACOG Committee Opinion No. 460, "The Initial Reproductive Health Visit."

What the first visit is actually like

The first visit is mostly a conversation, not an exam. The clinician talks with your daughter about general health, body changes, menstrual cycle, and questions she may have. It's a chance for her to develop a relationship with a women's health provider and start understanding her own body in a safe space.

A general physical (height, weight, blood pressure) and an external genital exam are usually included. A pelvic exam (the internal exam) is not routinely done at the first visit unless there's a specific concern such as severe pain, abnormal bleeding, or possible infection.

The first Pap test

The first Pap test is recommended at age 21, regardless of when sexual activity began. So your 13 to 15 year old will not have a Pap test at her first visit.

Source: ACOG Practice Bulletin No. 168.

About confidentiality

Adolescent patients have meaningful confidentiality protections. Topics like sexual activity, contraception, sexually transmitted infections, and certain mental health concerns can be kept confidential between the patient and the clinician, with limited exceptions for safety. Many practices, including ours, take time alone with the teen during part of the visit so she can ask anything.

For parents, this can feel uncomfortable, but research shows it builds trust and makes teens significantly more likely to share honest information that helps their health.

Source: Society for Adolescent Health and Medicine Position Statement on Confidentiality, 2016.

How to prepare your daughter

  • Tell her the visit is mostly a conversation, no pelvic exam unless needed
  • Let her know she can ask anything, even things she might not want to ask you
  • Remind her she can decline anything she's not comfortable with
  • Bring her insurance card and a list of medications or supplements
  • If she has a regular period, the date of her last one is helpful

What we do

Our adolescent care follows ACOG guidance closely. We focus on listening, building trust, and providing accurate information. The first visit usually takes 30 to 40 minutes.

What to expect at your first annual gynecology visit

A clear walk through of what happens at the annual well woman exam, what's typically covered, and how to make the most of your visit.

If you've never been to a gynecologist before, or it's been a long time, the first visit can feel uncertain. Here is exactly what happens, why each part matters, and how to make the visit work for you.

Before you arrive

Bring your insurance card, a photo ID, and a list of any medications including vitamins or supplements. If you can, note the date of your last period and any pattern changes you've noticed. Wear something comfortable, ideally with separate top and bottom for easier changing.

The conversation

The visit starts with a conversation, not an exam. Your clinician will ask about your health history, menstrual cycle, any symptoms, sexual history, contraception, family medical history, and lifestyle. There are no judgments here. Everything you share is confidential and is what your clinician needs in order to give you good care.

The physical exam

A standard annual exam includes height, weight, and blood pressure. Then a clinical breast exam looks for lumps, skin changes, or other concerns. The pelvic exam comes next, which has three parts: a look at the vulva, an internal look at the vagina and cervix using a tool called a speculum, and a bimanual exam (one hand on your abdomen, two gloved fingers in the vagina) to feel the size and position of the uterus and ovaries.

The Pap test

If you are 21 or older and due for screening, you'll have a Pap test during the pelvic exam. ACOG recommends Pap testing every three years between ages 21 and 29, and from age 30 to 65 you can choose between a Pap every three years, an HPV test every five years, or both together every five years.

Source: ACOG Practice Bulletin No. 168 (Cervical Cancer Screening).

What about a mammogram?

For most women, screening mammograms start at age 40. If you have a family history of breast cancer or other risk factors, your clinician may recommend starting earlier.

Source: USPSTF Breast Cancer Screening Recommendations, 2024.

STI testing

The CDC recommends annual chlamydia and gonorrhea testing for sexually active women under 25 and for older women with risk factors. HIV testing is recommended at least once between ages 13 and 64. You can request additional testing at any time.

Source: CDC STI Treatment Guidelines, 2021.

Vaccinations and health counseling

Depending on your age and history, the visit might include a flu shot, an HPV vaccine catch up if you missed it earlier, or discussion of bone density screening. Your clinician will also talk through general health: blood pressure, weight, diet, alcohol, smoking if relevant, and mental health.

Questions worth asking

The annual is your appointment, not a checklist. Common things to ask about:

  • Birth control: anything new on the market, any concerns about what you're using
  • Menstrual changes, pain, or heavy bleeding
  • Sex: comfort, desire, pain, satisfaction
  • Pregnancy planning, fertility, or pregnancy prevention
  • Menopause symptoms if you are in your 40s or 50s
  • Anything that worries you, no matter how small

How long does it take?

About 30 to 45 minutes. We schedule visits to allow real conversation. You should not feel rushed.

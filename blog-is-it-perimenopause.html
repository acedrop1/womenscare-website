Is it perimenopause? 7 evidence based signs you should know

Seven research backed signs of perimenopause, with the studies behind each. From cycle changes and hot flushes to joint aches and brain fog.

If you're in your 40s or even your late 30s and feeling like something has shifted, you're not imagining it. Perimenopause, the transition before menopause, can begin years before periods actually stop. Below are seven evidence based signs to know, with the research behind them.

1. Periods that change without warning

One of the earliest and most common signs of perimenopause is a change in the menstrual cycle. Cycles may get shorter, longer, heavier, lighter, or skip months entirely. A 2017 study published in Menopause, the journal of the North American Menopause Society, found that variability in cycle length is often the first measurable signal of the transition.

Source: Harlow SD et al. "Executive Summary of the Stages of Reproductive Aging Workshop +10." Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism, 2012.

2. Hot flushes and night sweats

Often called vasomotor symptoms, hot flushes affect up to 80 percent of women during the menopause transition according to NAMS. They can range from a brief warm feeling to drenching night sweats that wake you up. They typically peak during the late perimenopause and the first few years after the final menstrual period.

Source: NAMS Position Statement on Vasomotor Symptoms, 2023.

3. Sleep that won't come

Difficulty falling asleep, staying asleep, or waking unrefreshed is a common perimenopausal complaint. Some of this is driven by night sweats, but oestrogen also influences sleep architecture directly. Research from the Study of Women's Health Across the Nation (SWAN) shows that sleep disruption increases substantially during perimenopause.

Source: Kravitz HM et al. SWAN Sleep Study. Sleep, 2008.

4. Mood shifts, irritability, and brain fog

Many women describe feeling more anxious, more easily irritated, or just less sharp. ACOG has acknowledged a real connection between perimenopausal hormone changes and mood. A 2018 meta analysis in JAMA Psychiatry found a meaningfully increased risk of depressive symptoms during the menopause transition compared with premenopause.

Source: Maki PM et al. "Guidelines for the evaluation and treatment of perimenopausal depression." Menopause, 2018.

5. Joint aches

Less talked about, but well documented. Oestrogen helps regulate inflammation in joints. As levels decline, many women notice new aches, especially in hips, knees, and hands. This is sometimes called the "menopause arthritis" pattern, though it is not technically arthritis.

Source: Watt FE. "Musculoskeletal pain and menopause." Post Reproductive Health, 2018.

6. Sex feels different

Lower oestrogen affects vaginal tissue, leading to dryness or discomfort. Some women also notice changes in libido. These are real, common, and treatable. They are not something you simply have to accept.

Source: NAMS Position Statement on Genitourinary Syndrome of Menopause, 2020.

7. New urinary symptoms

Urinary urgency, more frequent infections, or stress incontinence (leakage with coughing or exercise) can begin or worsen during this transition. The same oestrogen decline that affects vaginal tissue also affects the urethra.

Source: American Urogynecologic Society Position Statement, 2020.

What you can do

None of these symptoms have to be tolerated in silence. There are evidence based treatments for each one, from lifestyle changes to non hormonal options to hormone therapy, depending on what fits your history.
